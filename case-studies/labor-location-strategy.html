CreateMe — Maximizing Talent Access and Relocation Impact

San Francisco Bay Area · Labor Catchment & Commute Analysis

CreateMe needed to open a prototype factory in the East and South Bay. The goal wasn't just finding available industrial space — it was finding the right location within reasonable distance of their headquarters that would maximize access to a highly specialized engineering talent pool while minimizing attrition risk to their existing workforce. A poorly-sited facility could simultaneously fail to attract new talent and accelerate turnover among the people they already had.

The Approach

We conducted a workforce composition assessment customized to CreateMe's specific occupational requirements — identifying the precise SOC codes that mapped to their engineering needs and quantifying where those workers actually lived across the Bay Area. We built a labor catchment model to identify which specific locations within their target geography would capture the highest concentration of their target talent within a defined drive time. A simultaneous commute analysis and time-optimized center of gravity study measured the inter-site travel burden the new facility would create for existing employees who needed to move between headquarters and the new location — a cost that's rarely modeled and almost always underestimated.

The facility sited on rent economics alone would have been in the wrong place. The analysis moved the decision from a map to a model — and the model pointed somewhere different.

Results

Optimal facility locations identified balancing engineering talent access and existing employee retention
Labor catchment model delivered identifying highest-concentration talent zones by drive time
Commute burden quantified for inter-site travel — a factor most location analyses ignore entirely
Client focused real estate search on specific geographies with demonstrated workforce advantage

Tradecraft Industries — Market Identification and Micro-Location Analysis

Multi-Market Expansion · Denver-Based Company

Tradecraft had built a successful business at their primary location in Denver and wanted to replicate that success in additional markets. The challenge was that "what made Denver work" wasn't obvious — and replicating it required first understanding the underlying drivers of their first location's success before identifying where those conditions existed elsewhere.

The Approach

We developed a two-phase analysis. The first phase identified target markets using a detailed ranking and weighting system built around macro indicators relevant to Tradecraft's business model — migration and population growth, cost of living, trade industries business presence, unionization levels, and target demographic concentration. We screened all major U.S. markets and eliminated those falling below the median on the combined customer service and field operations opportunity scores. The second phase went deeper — simulating drive-time trade areas around every potential block group in shortlisted markets, scoring each block for its probability of meeting the defined criteria. This gave Tradecraft a neighborhood-level view of where to focus, not just a city.

Results

Market screening completed across all major U.S. metros using macro and micro indicators
Block-group-level opportunity scoring delivered for shortlisted markets
Drive-time trade area simulation identified precise sub-market opportunity zones
Client's real estate search narrowed from national to a targeted set of high-confidence locations

Hormel — Workforce Assessment and Hiring Feasibility

Midwest Production Facility · Labor Supply & Wage Analysis

Hormel's ham-boning plant needed to hire 120 production workers within three months. Attrition had risen to 30–40%, federal stimulus was compressing the effective wage gap between working and not working, and management was genuinely unsure whether the location could attract enough labor at any wage — or whether consolidating or relocating operations was the only viable path forward.

The Approach

We conducted a workforce assessment and wage feasibility analysis designed to answer one specific question: is it possible for Hormel to attract enough qualified labor at this location by raising wages, or is the supply problem structural? We interviewed hiring managers at the five largest regional employers competing for similar positions, as well as economic development stakeholders with direct visibility into regional hiring conditions. We benchmarked occupational wage rates at the 25th, average, and 75th percentile levels across relevant SOC codes for the target labor types. In-depth labor supply and demand modeling was performed across drive-time geographies from the facility.

Results

Confirmed sufficient labor supply existed to support expansion at the current location with wage adjustment
Wage benchmarking by percentile delivered for all target occupations
Regional employer interviews conducted to triangulate data with on-the-ground intelligence
Hormel expanded in place and revised marketing, retention, and compensation strategies based on findings

Lyte — Market Opportunity Analysis for Remote-First Expansion

National Market Screening · 380 Markets Evaluated

Lyte was a rapidly growing start-up operating primarily remote, with 140 employees expected by year-end and 180 by the following year. Leadership had decided the company needed one or more central offices to accommodate growth and foster a new stage of organizational culture — but they had no consensus on where. Some markets were intuitively appealing; others hadn't been considered at all. They needed an objective framework before making a multi-year commitment.

The Approach

We built a multivariate market selection model benchmarking all major U.S. metros across labor availability, wage levels, economic dynamism in the company's target industry, workforce diversity, airport and travel connectivity, office rents, and core demographic indicators. After eliminating markets falling below the median on the combined opportunity scoring, 380 markets were narrowed to a focused shortlist. Each remaining market was scored on a combined Opportunity Index incorporating both customer service and field operations labor metrics. The model validated certain markets leadership had already identified as favorable — and surfaced several high-performing markets that had not been on their radar at all.

Results

380 U.S. markets screened and scored across 12 weighted variables
Shortlist of high-confidence markets delivered for further sub-market exploration
Analysis validated pre-existing assumptions while surfacing new opportunities not previously considered
Client had the data confidence needed to commit to a new market for physical expansion

Standard Bots — Labor Availability Analysis, Metro NYC

New York Metro Area · Block-Group-Level Labor Mapping

Standard Bots needed to relocate from their current location and required a precise understanding of where their target workforce — specialized manufacturing and assembly technicians — actually lived within Metro New York before committing to a new address. In a market as geographically complex as the New York metro, the difference between Kings County, Queens, Nassau, and Suffolk represents dramatically different labor pools, wage profiles, and commute dynamics.

The Approach

We identified the precise occupational classifications (SOC codes) for Standard Bots' target labor — both the precise and broader categories of electrical, electronic, and electromechanical assemblers, engine assemblers, timing device assemblers, and related technician roles. We then mapped the residential concentration of that labor at the block-group (neighborhood) level across the full metro area, producing a county-by-county summary of target labor resident workers, employment, five-year projected change, and wage distribution (25th percentile, average, and 75th percentile hourly earnings). Heat maps visualized where the highest concentrations of target labor lived, providing a clear spatial framework for evaluating specific building options.

Results

County-level labor summary delivered across Kings, Queens, Nassau, and Suffolk counties
Block-group-level heat maps produced showing precise residential concentration of target workers
Wage benchmarking by percentile delivered for all target SOC classifications
Client narrowed location search to a small number of high-confidence submarkets based on labor data

Casa Padel — Customer Market Opportunity Analysis

National Market Screening · Sports & Recreation Expansion

Casa Padel was positioned to be a first-mover in introducing padel — the fastest-growing racket sport in the world — to U.S. markets through physical facility expansion. The challenge was that padel had essentially no U.S. track record, meaning there were no existing locations to run regression analysis against. The site selection model had to be built from first principles: who plays padel globally, what demographic and lifestyle characteristics predict participation, and which U.S. markets have the highest concentration of those characteristics?

The Approach

Working directly with Casa Padel's founders to establish and weight the selection criteria, we developed an Opportunity Index built around the demographic profile of existing padel, racquetball, and tennis players. Key inputs included target household income, geographic concentration of the target demographic (including Latino/Hispanic population, a strong predictor of padel participation internationally), cost of living, concentration of racket sports participation, and projected population growth. We screened all 380 major U.S. metros, scored each, and produced a ranked national market list paired with geographic maps showing where the highest-scoring concentrations existed within each top market.

Results

380 U.S. markets scored and ranked on a custom-weighted Opportunity Index
Shortlist of high-confidence expansion markets delivered with full scoring methodology
Demographic and lifestyle variables identified as predictors of padel participation in absence of U.S. data
Client had an objective, board-ready framework for prioritizing market entry sequence
