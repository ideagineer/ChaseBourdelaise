Gebrüder Weiss — Industrial Site Selection to Optimize Distribution

National Site Selection · 3PL · 200,000+ SF Distribution with Temperature Control

Gebrüder Weiss — the oldest third-party logistics provider in the world, founded in 1823 — needed to consolidate bifurcated distribution facilities representing over 200,000 square feet of space, including a temperature-controlled component, into a single optimized location. The company had strict service level agreements with its customers governing 1-day, 2-day, and 3-day ground shipping coverage. Finding a location that met those commitments while also minimizing labor cost and real estate expense required a fundamentally different analytical approach than traditional site selection.

The Challenge

Standard industrial site selection — market, submarket, building — doesn't account for the network effect of logistics. A building that's cheap to lease can be wildly expensive to operate if it adds half a day to average delivery times or sits outside the service radius of key ground shipping hubs. For a 3PL with SLA-bound commitments, the cost of the wrong location isn't measured in rent per square foot — it's measured in penalty clauses and lost contracts.

The Approach

We developed a custom screening model built specifically around Gebrüder Weiss's service requirements. The model mapped population access within 1, 2, and 3-day ground shipping zones from each candidate market, overlaid the locations of FedEx and UPS ground hubs to ensure proximity to the physical network, and integrated labor availability and wage cost data within 60 minutes of each candidate hub. Real estate cost data was then overlaid as a final variable — so that locations were sorted by their ability to meet service requirements first, and their cost efficiency second. The result was a ranked scorecard of candidate markets with a clear basis for recommendation.

The client's team had not been exposed to this level of data-driven real estate analysis. The approach earned multiple additional expansion assignments across the US and Canada — the start of an ongoing portfolio and transaction strategy relationship.

Results

Custom screening model built to optimize for 1/2/3-day ground shipping SLA coverage nationally
FedEx and UPS hub proximity integrated as a hard constraint, not a secondary consideration
Labor availability and wage cost modeled within 60-minute drive of all candidate hub locations
Ranked market recommendation delivered with full analytical basis — client awarded multiple follow-on expansion assignments across US and Canada

Empire Moulding & Millwork — Logistics Analysis for Lease Renewal Decision

Illinois · Stay vs. Relocate · Supply Chain Cost Modeling

Empire Moulding & Millwork, a Novo Building Products company, had an Illinois lease expiring within two years. Before committing to a renewal, leadership wanted an objective answer to a specific question: would relocating to a different facility reduce their logistics costs enough to justify the disruption and expense of a move? The company had a defined set of existing shipping destinations and a clear sense of where their customers were — the analysis needed to translate location options into total supply chain cost.

The Challenge

Most lease renewal decisions are made primarily on real estate economics — rent, TIA, term. Empire's leadership understood that for a manufacturing and distribution business, the cost of the building is often less important than the cost of moving product from it. A location two hours away might look better on a rent comparison while costing significantly more on an all-in logistics basis. The analysis needed to make those total costs visible and comparable before anyone signed anything.

The Approach

We performed a supply chain analysis using an optimized location-allocation model to calculate the total cost exposure of each candidate location — incorporating trucking distance, trucking time, total sales maximization, and sales margin impact. Four scenarios were modeled: the current location, a trucking-distance-optimized center of gravity, an unweighted center of gravity, and a trucking-time-and-margin-weighted center of gravity. Labor availability and crime index scores were integrated across all sites. The analysis identified a location approximately two hours from the current facility that would reduce trucking miles by 4.2% and hours by 5.0%. However, the full model also revealed that the labor availability advantage at the current location significantly outweighed the logistics savings.

Results

Supply chain cost model built across four location scenarios — trucking distance, time, margin, and combined
Optimal logistical center of gravity identified approximately 2 hours from current location
Full analysis concluded that labor availability advantage at current location outweighed logistics savings from relocation
Client proceeded with Illinois renewal with full financial justification — not just intuition — behind the decision

Tranzonic — West Coast Portfolio Consolidation

Western U.S. · Multi-Facility Consolidation · Transportation, Drayage, Labor & Rent Modeling

Tranzonic was seeking to consolidate its West Coast distribution operations — multiple facilities across the western time zone — into a single location that would simplify logistics, reduce drayage costs, and lower combined rent and LTL/TL transportation expense. The question wasn't simply "which city has the cheapest industrial space" — it was "which single location produces the lowest total cost of operations when transportation, labor, drayage, and rent are all accounted for simultaneously."

The Approach

We quantified Tranzonic's current West Coast cost exposure across all four factors — transportation logistics, drayage, labor, and rent — establishing a baseline total cost for the existing distributed portfolio. We then modeled the same four-factor cost profile for multiple candidate consolidation sites across the western time zone. Each candidate site was evaluated under current volume conditions and under two growth scenarios (20% and 30% growth) to ensure the recommendation would hold as the business scaled. Warehouse labor residence maps within 30-minute drive times were produced for each alternative site to validate labor availability assumptions. The analysis identified Las Vegas, NV and Lebec, CA as the top consolidation candidates.

Results

Four-factor cost model (transportation, drayage, labor, rent) built and benchmarked across all current West Coast locations
Multiple consolidation candidate sites evaluated under current volume and two growth scenarios
Las Vegas, NV and Lebec, CA identified as optimal single-consolidation candidates
Labor availability verified at each candidate site via 30-minute drive-time workforce mapping
Consolidated site confirmed to reduce total West Coast cost exposure relative to distributed portfolio at growth trajectory
